Quite a few (countable), quite a number of, quite a lot

  1. We've got a few Americans working here.
  2. I'd like to order another one hundred boxes of red wine
  3. We sold quite a number of them last summer
  4. They had quite a bit of money in their client's account
  5. Don't worry, there's quite a bit of time left yet
  6. I've been to Japan quite a bit this year (often)
  7. I've been studying English for quite a while now (a long time)
  8. A: How long are you intending to stay here? B: I'm not sure but probaly quite a while.

Ages (a very long time)

  1. I meet your brother quite a bit these days but I haven't seen your sister in/for ages
    * In: would only be used after a negative or superlative construction
    * But not in the future. we would not say: we won't arrive in ages.
  2. It's seem ages since I was last here.

Well off (rich)

  1. He's so well off, he doesn't know what to spend his money on.
  2. If I were as well off as she is, I'd retire.
  3. Young people these days dont know how well off they are.
    * Well off does not necessary have to refer to oney; it can also be used in respect of oppotunities, facilities, etc.
  4. He is far better off than I am.
    * "Better off" is a comparative form of "well off"
    * However it can also be used to mean " It would be better for ..."
  5. You'd better off going the motorway than taking a country route, if you want to arrive there by 6 o'clock
  6. She's better off without her husband, he made her life a misery.
  7. I'd better fo now, I've got quite a few things to do. * "had better" is a stronger form of "should"
  8. Even though my brother has been working for quite a number of years, he is worse off than me.
    * the opposite of "better off" is "worse off"

To hear of (to be familiar with/to know the existence/to regconise ...)

  1. Have you ever hear of a company called ABC limited?
  2. I'm surprised you've never heard of him, he's quite well off.
  3. Before we came to London, we had never heard of this type of crime.

To hear about

  1. A: Have you heard about John? -B: No, tell me -A: Unfortunately, he has lost his legs.
    * To hear, find out what has happened to someone
  2. I found this school in the Yellow Pages. How did you hear about/of it?
    * to find out about something
  3. I was very happy to hear about/of the recent addition to your family
    * hear of == hear about for this meaning only
  4. As long as we have met; I've heard quite a bit about you.
    * To receive information about someone/sth from somebody else, e.g reputation, character. ...
  5. I'm surprised you haven't heard more about our company; we've got quite a reputation.
    * if using "haven't heard of": not even know the company exists.

To hear from (to receive news, communication from someone)

  1. I haven't heard from him in ages.
  2. When was the last time you heard from your daughter? -Oh, quite a while ago.

Fussy (Difficult to please, not easily satisfied) /ˈfʌs.i/

  1. Tell me what you want to eat before I make dinner because I know you're a fussy eater/how fussy you are.
  2. She's not very fussy about whom she goes out with, providing he's well-off.

To make a fuss (to complain, to be difficult)

  2. I do wish you'd stop making a fuss and continue with your work.
  3. My dogs loves to make a fuss of/fussed over

  4. We've stayed in quite a few hotels recently where the staff made too much fuss of us.

    * I can/could do without can also mean: I wish I didn't have or I wish I hadn't had (I could have done without)
  6. I couldn't have done without your assitance; you're so clumsy!

To push in (to go in front of someone in a queue or waiting list without permission)

  1. Sorry to be bossy, but would you mind not pushing in? I've been waiting in this queue for ages!
  2. How did you manage to get treated by the doctor before anyone else? - Well, I must own up; I did a bit of pushing in.

To go down well (to be welcomed, well received, accepted - usually a decision, piece of news, a performer or a person who is introduced to others fr the first time)

  1. the company's decision to ban smoking in the office was a turn up for the books and didn't go down well with most of employees.
  2. I'm surprised to hear that such a typical English actor went down so well all over France.
  3. She married a well-off lawyer, didn't she? That/he must have gone down well with her parents!
  4. How do you think it'll go down with your clients?
    * Note that one can use this expression in a question without a word "well"

To go/come down with (to catch an illness - e.g. the flu, a cold)

  1. Why has he got the day off? - I think he has gone down with the flu/a cold.
  2. I don't feel up to going to the cinema. I'm coming down with something*
    * Not anything serious.

To go down as (to be remember by everone in a certain way, often be used in the future tense)

  1. Last year will go down as one of the most appaling in the company's history.
  2. This treaty will go down (in history) as being the first major breakthrough in peace negotiations between the super powers.
