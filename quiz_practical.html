Quite a few (countable), quite a number of, quite a lot

  1. We've got a few Americans working here.
  2. I'd like to order another one hundred boxes of red wine
  3. We sold quite a number of them last summer
  4. They had quite a bit of money in their client's account
  5. Don't worry, there's quite a bit of time left yet
  6. I've been to Japan quite a bit this year (often)
  7. I've been studying English for quite a while now (a long time)
  8. A: How long are you intending to stay here? B: I'm not sure but probaly quite a while.

Ages (a very long time)

  1. I meet your brother quite a bit these days but I haven't seen your sister in/for ages
    * In: would only be used after a negative or superlative construction * But not in the future. we would not say: we won't arrive in ages.
  2. It's seem ages since I was last here.

Well off (rich)

  1. He's so well of, he doesn't know what to spend his money on.
  2. If I were as well off as she is, I'd retire.
  3. Young people these days dont know how well off they are.
    * Well off does not necessary have to refer to oney; it can also be used in respect of oppotunities, facilities, etc.
  4. He is far better off than I am.
    * "Better off" is a comparative form of "well off" * However it can also be used to mean " It would be better for ..."
  5. You'd better off going the motorway than taking a country route, if you want to arrive there by 6 o'clock
  6. She's better off without her husband, he made her life a misery.
  7. I'd better fo now, I've got quite a few things to do. * "had better" is a stronger form of "should"
  8. Even though my brother has been working for quite a number of years, he is worse off than me.
    * the opposite of "better off" is "worse off"

Fussy (Difficult to please, not easily satisfied) /ˈfʌs.i/

  1. Tell me what you want to eat before I make dinner because I know you're a fussy eater/how fussy you are.
  2. She's not very fussy about whom she goes out with, providing he's well-off.
    * choosy, picky, particular: have the same meaning as fussy

To make a fuss (to complain, to be difficult)

  1. If I'm in a restaurant and the service is slow, I can't help making a fuss.
  2. I do wish you'd stop making a fuss and continue with your work.
  3. My dogs loves to make a fuss of/fussed over
    * Do not confuse this expression to "make a fuss of/to fuss over" which means to show affection towards a person or animal, to give someone a lot of care and attention.
  4. We've stayed in quite a few hotels recently where the staff made too much fuss of us.

Stale (unfresh)

  1. Waiter, sorry to make a fuss, but this bread is stale.
  2. I think I'd better open the window in the conference room; it's a bit stale in there.
  3. Can you remind me of how the company's accounts system works? I'm sorry, my mind is a little stale on this matter
  4. It's clear that we've reached a stalemate; we'd better off starting again.
    * The word "stalemate" describes a situation where neither side in a dispute or negotiations can progress or win

To go off

  1. This milk smells like it has gone off/it's off.
    * to go bad
  2. Don't be so fussy! I'm sure those sausages haven't gone off/aren't off.
  3. My alarm clock went off an hour earlier than it was supposed to this morning.
    * to ring (alarm)
  4. You'd better off without a car alarm; they're always going off at the wrong time.
  5. Have you heard about that bomb which went off in the city centre yesterday?
    * to explode, to fire, ie a bomb or a gun
  6. He claims that the gun went off accidentally, but I've heard about his pasts and I'm a bit suspicious.
  7. I've gone off her coffee. I think we'd better off if she just offered us tea.
    * to stop liking somthing or someone (colloquial)
  8. She went off her last boyfriend after hearing about his past relationship.
  9. I'm going off the idea of doing business with them, they're far too fussy.
    * I'm changing my mind about ...
  10. Quite a number of letters went off this morning but they won't arrive for ages because of the strike.
    * to be sent - a letter or a fax - often used in the office
  11. I'm sorry, you've missed her, she went off to work quite a while ago.
    * to leave a particular place, to travel around
  12. I heard about your journey, you went off round the world, didn't you?
  13. This rooms smells stale because the air conditioning went off during the night.
    * to be switchted off, to stop working- usually somthing powered by electricity or gas
    * comparing with "... the air conditioning is off", which means that it was not switched on in the first place

To get one's own back on someone, to get someone back (to get one's revenge)

  1. It's no use making a fuss of him. He still wants to get his own back on you/to get you back for what you did to him last week.
  2. I've heard that the bomb which went off near the police station was planned by a terrorist organisation, trying to get their own back for recent arrests.
  3. I'm sorry, I don't have the details on me now. Would you mind if I got/came back to you later?
    * Compare with "to get/come back to someone on/with something", which means to come back to someone on a particular matter or with the information they require.
  4. Can you get/come back to me with their phone number this afternoon? I want to make a fuss about the stale food they sent us.

May/might as well

  1. We may as well go to John's place again unless you can think of something more interesting to do.
    * One should do something only because there is nothing better to do (negative feeling about it)
  2. Shall we go off to work now? -I suppose we might as well.
  3. Do you think I should take a pullover with me to Greece? - Well, probably you won't need it but you might as well take it just in case.
    * There's no harm in, it's worthwhile doing something
  4. Eventhough I've quite a number of dictionaries at home, I might as well buy this one as it's so cheap.
  5. As the weather is so hot and sunny this year, I've gone off the idea of going abroad; I may as wll stay in England.
    * No difference in doing somthings, and one should choose an easier or cheaper thing.
  6. The office party was so boring, I might as wll have remained at home.
    * the past of may/might as well is may/might as well have + past participle

To do up

  1. I don't want to make a fuss, but you'd better do your shirt up before meeting the boss.
    * to tie up, to button up, faten up, zip up
  2. Do you want me to give you a hand in doing up your dress? - Yes, you may as well.
  3. Would you mind if I undid my jacket? Your shoelaces are undone
    * The opposite of to do up is to undo
  4. I we don't go away this summer, we might as well do the house up.
    * to decorate, renovate
  5. It's been ages since we last had our house done up.

to take after (to be similar to an elder memeber of the same family, usually in character rather than in appearance)

  1. My daughter likes to be made a fuss of; she takes after me in that respect.
  2. You take after your grandfather; he also used to go off people quickly.

to bet (to be almost certain something will or will not happen) (colloquial)

  1. I bet you won't be able to do up your bed yourself. I wouldn't bet on it!
  2. I bet 10$ on Liperfool winning the cup this year.
  3. I'll get my own back on you one day! -Do you want a bet?
    * Do you want a bet?: informal expression for challenging something someone else has just said

What's the point? (What's the use/purpose, what for?)

  1. What's the point in/of coming to England if you don't want to meet English people? You may as well stay in your own country.
  2. Shall we employ another Accounts Clerk?- What's the point? We've already got quite a few.
  3. There's no point in speaking to him, he takes after his father, in that he never listens to anything anyone says.
    * We can also use "point" in a negative form with "There is"; i.e. it's useless, a waste of time
    * What the point you are trying to make? == what are you trying to say?

To tread (to step on something) on/in (liquid)

  1. When I was in the army, I had to be very careful to to tread on unexploded bombs in case they went off.
  2. You've just trodden on my clean floor with your dirty shoes. I'll get you back on this!
  3. Some children enjoy treading in puddles after it has been raining.
  4. Just because she's well-off, she thinks she can tread on everyone
    * figurative sense with people, meaning to treat (general attitiute, not action) people as thought they do not matter
    * We would not say: she trod on me yesterday

Overdraft, to be/go overdrawn

  1. Instead of relying on your overdraft facility, you'd better off taking aout a loan.
  2. I bet she won't be able to clear her overdraft by the end of the year.
  3. It has come to our attention that your account is overdrawn in the sum of 2000$
    * the phrase "in the sum of" is often used in formal letters concerning money, in spoken English on would normally use "by"
  4. I'm overdrawn.

to look forward to (+ gerund) ( wait with pleasure)

  1. I was really looking forward to seeing Alice. I haven't heard from her in ages.
  2. I'm not looking forward to receiving my bank statement; I must be overdrawn by at leat $3400.
    i.e. something's going to happen, the bank statement is definiteky going to arrive and I'm not happy about it
  3. I'm looking forward to hearing from you soon.
    * don't be confuse with "look for": I'm looking for my glasses I hope no one has trodden on them.
  4. I'm not looking forward to going out tonight
    * This means the person is going out, but would rather not

Day off (a day's holiday)

  1. Can we get back to you tomorrow? The person dealing with this matter has got the day off today.
  2. I have two extra days off next month. I'm really looking forward to them!
    * One should note that although people often use a "week off", "a month off", "a year off". Off-week and off-month are not so common.
  3. I would have bet a million pounds on her passing her exams but she failed. She must have an off-day.
    * do not confuse "day off" with "off-day", which means a bad day or a day in which one does not perform as well as on usually does.
    * "off-day" must be used with "have". One cannot say: It's an off-day.
  4. The captain of the football team is having an off-day, he might as well not have played.

To show off (to show that one is very proud of one's abilities, achievements or possessions, to boast)

  1. I don't want to show off but I must tell you that I came first in all my exams when I was at the university.
  2. Just because I'm having an off-day today, it doesn't mean you have to show off in front of everyone watching.
  3. It's surprising his friends haven't gone off him, after the way he keeps showing off his new car.
  4. I'm looking forward to getting my own back on that show-off
