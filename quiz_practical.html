Quite a few (countable), quite a number of, quite a lot

  1. We've got a few Americans working here.
  2. I'd like to order another one hundred boxes of red wine
  3. We sold quite a number of them last summer
  4. They had quite a bit of money in their client's account
  5. Don't worry, there's quite a bit of time left yet
  6. I've been to Japan quite a bit this year (often)
  7. I've been studying English for quite a while now (a long time)
  8. A: How long are you intending to stay here? B: I'm not sure but probaly quite a while.

Ages (a very long time)

  1. I meet your brother quite a bit these days but I haven't seen your sister in/for ages
    * In: would only be used after a negative or superlative construction * But not in the future. we would not say: we won't arrive in ages.
  2. It's seem ages since I was last here.

Well off (rich)

  1. He's so well of, he doesn't know what to spend his money on.
  2. If I were as well off as she is, I'd retire.
  3. Young people these days dont know how well off they are.
    * Well off does not necessary have to refer to oney; it can also be used in respect of oppotunities, facilities, etc.
  4. He is far better off than I am.
    * "Better off" is a comparative form of "well off" * However it can also be used to mean " It would be better for ..."
  5. You'd better off going the motorway than taking a country route, if you want to arrive there by 6 o'clock
  6. She's better off without her husband, he made her life a misery.
  7. I'd better fo now, I've got quite a few things to do. * "had better" is a stronger form of "should"
  8. Even though my brother has been working for quite a number of years, he is worse off than me.
    * the opposite of "better off" is "worse off"

Fussy (Difficult to please, not easily satisfied) /ˈfʌs.i/

  1. Tell me what you want to eat before I make dinner because I know you're a fussy eater/how fussy you are.
  2. She's not very fussy about whom she goes out with, providing he's well-off.
    * choosy, picky, particular: have the same meaning as fussy

To make a fuss (to complain, to be difficult)

  1. If I'm in a restaurant and the service is slow, I can't help making a fuss.
  2. I do wish you'd stop making a fuss and continue with your work.
  3. My dogs loves to make a fuss of/fussed over
    * Do not confuse this expression to "make a fuss of/to fuss over" which means to show affection towards a person or animal, to give someone a lot of care and attention.
  4. We've stayed in quite a few hotels recently where the staff made too much fuss of us.

Stale (unfresh)

  1. Waiter, sorry to make a fuss, but this bread is stale.
  2. I think I'd better open the window in the conference room; it's a bit stale in there.
  3. Can you remind me of how the company's accounts system works? I'm sorry, my mind is a little stale on this matter
  4. It's clear that we've reached a stalemate; we'd better off starting again.
    * The word "stalemate" describes a situation where neither side in a dispute or negotiations can progress or win

To go off

  1. This milk smells like it has gone off/it's off.
    * to go bad
  2. Don't be so fussy! I'm sure those sausages haven't gone off/aren't off.
  3. My alarm clock went off an hour earlier than it was supposed to this morning.
    * to ring (alarm)
  4. You'd better off without a car alarm; they're always going off at the wrong time.
  5. Have you heard about that bomb which went off in the city centre yesterday?
    * to explode, to fire, ie a bomb or a gun
  6. He claims that the gun went off accidentally, but I've heard about his pasts and I'm a bit suspicious.
  7. I've gone off her coffee. I think we'd better off if she just offered us tea.
    * to stop liking somthing or someone (colloquial)
  8. She went off her last boyfriend after hearing about his past relationship.
  9. I'm going off the idea of doing business with them, they're far too fussy.
    * I'm changing my mind about ...
  10. Quite a number of letters went off this morning but they won't arrive for ages because of the strike.
    * to be sent - a letter or a fax - often used in the office
  11. I'm sorry, you've missed her, she went off to work quite a while ago.
    * to leave a particular place, to travel around
  12. I heard about your journey, you went off round the world, didn't you?
  13. This rooms smells stale because the air conditioning went off during the night.
    * to be switchted off, to stop working- usually somthing powered by electricity or gas
    * comparing with "... the air conditioning is off", which means that it was not switched on in the first place

To get one's own back on someone, to get someone back (to get one's revenge)

  1. It's no use making a fuss of him. He still wants to get his own back on you/to get you back for what you did to him last week.
  2. I've heard that the bomb which went off near the police station was planned by a terrorist organisation, trying to get their own back for recent arrests.
  3. I'm sorry, I don't have the details on me now. Would you mind if I got/came back to you later?
    * Compare with "to get/come back to someone on/with something", which means to come back to someone on a particular matter or with the information they require.
  4. Can you get/come back to me with their phone number this afternoon? I want to make a fuss about the stale food they sent us.
